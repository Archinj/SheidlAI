# ShieldAI — Customer Discovery Journey

How WordPress site owners discover, evaluate, and decide on security solutions.

---

## Overview

This document maps the five-stage journey a WordPress site owner takes from first noticing a security problem to becoming a paying ShieldAI customer. Understanding this journey determines where ShieldAI invests in content, community, and advertising.

---

## Trigger Events

The moment a site owner goes from passively accepting risk to actively searching for a solution. For security, triggers are almost always reactive rather than proactive.

**Trigger 1 — The Hack or Near-Miss**
Their site gets flagged by Google, defaced, or their host suspends the account. This is the highest-urgency trigger. They are in panic mode and will buy within hours.

**Trigger 2 — The Scary Email**
Wordfence or another plugin sends an alert about a critical vulnerability in an installed plugin. They read it, don't understand it, and feel anxious but don't know what to do.

**Trigger 3 — The News Story**
A major WordPress vulnerability makes the news (e.g., a popular plugin with millions of installs has a 0-day). They suddenly think "I probably have that plugin."

**Trigger 4 — The Client Site Incident**
For freelancers and agencies: a client site gets compromised. Now security is a professional liability issue, not just a personal one.

**Trigger 5 — The Upgrade Moment**
A store owner whose WooCommerce revenue grows realizes "I actually have something to lose now." Security anxiety scales with revenue.

**Trigger 6 — The Audit**
A client, enterprise partner, or payment processor asks the site owner to demonstrate security practices. They have none documented.

## Stage 1: Problem Awareness

**What's happening:** The site owner knows something is wrong or could go wrong, but hasn't yet connected it to a specific solution.

**Feelings:** Anxiety, confusion, mild guilt ("I should have done something about this earlier").

**Behaviors:**
- Reads the scary email from Wordfence but doesn't click through
- Notices the site is slow and wonders if it's been hacked
- Sees a headline about WordPress vulnerabilities and worries
- Asks in a Facebook group: "How secure is my site?"

**What they're NOT doing yet:** Googling for security plugins. They haven't framed it as a solvable problem yet.

**Implication for ShieldAI:** Content that validates the anxiety and names the problem clearly wins at this stage. Blog posts like "Why Wordfence Emails Are So Confusing (And What To Do About Them)" reach people at this exact moment.

---

## Stage 2: Research

**What's happening:** They've decided they need a security solution. Now they're searching.

**Primary search queries:**
- "best WordPress security plugin 2024"
- "wordfence vs sucuri"
- "WordPress site hacked what to do"
- "how to fix WordPress security vulnerability"
- "WordPress security plugin free vs paid"
- "how to secure my WooCommerce store"
- "WordPress malware removal"
- "plugin vulnerability WordPress update"
- "WordPress hardening checklist"
- "what does Wordfence firewall alert mean"

**Where they search:**
- Google (primary)
- YouTube (looking for walkthroughs)
- Reddit r/Wordpress, r/woocommerce
- WPBeginner articles
- Facebook groups: "WordPress Help and Support," "WooCommerce Help"
- WordPress.org plugin reviews

**What they trust at this stage:**
- Top Google results from WPBeginner, Kinsta, WP Engine, WP Tavern
- YouTube tutorials from WPCrafter, WPTuts, Elegant Themes
- Reddit threads with upvoted community answers
- WordPress.org plugin page reviews (especially 1-star reviews for red flags)

**What they read:**
- Comparison articles: "Wordfence vs Sucuri vs [X]"
- "Best X plugins" roundups
- Negative reviews of current tools ("why I stopped using Wordfence")

**Implication for ShieldAI:** Must appear in comparison searches. Partner content on WPBeginner and Kinsta is disproportionately valuable. YouTube tutorials that show the approval queue UI in action will convert well.

---

## Stage 3: Evaluation

**What's happening:** They've identified 2-3 options and are comparing them.

**Evaluation criteria (in priority order):**
1. Does it actually fix the problem, or just alert me?
2. Will it break my site?
3. Is it easy to use? (Can I understand what it's doing?)
4. Is the price worth it?
5. Does it have good reviews and is it actively maintained?

**Key deal-breakers:**
- Confusing UI with no plain-English explanations
- No rollback option ("what if it breaks something?")
- "Auto-fix" language that feels scary and out of control
- Poor reviews mentioning false positives or site slowdown
- No free tier to try before buying

**How they evaluate:**
- Watch a YouTube demo/tutorial
- Read the WordPress.org plugin page reviews (especially recent ones)
- Look at active installs and last updated date
- Check if there's a free version they can test
- Ask in their Facebook group or Slack community

**ShieldAI's evaluation advantage:**
- Approval queue directly addresses "will it break my site?" fear
- Plain-English fix descriptions address the "I don't understand" concern
- Rollback feature is a strong reassurance point
- Live demo showing the queue UI will be compelling

---

## Stage 4: Decision

**What pushes them to buy:**
- A specific trigger event (hack, scary email, audit request)
- Seeing a clear before/after demo that makes the value tangible
- A friend or community member recommending it
- Annual pricing that feels like "only X per month" when broken down
- A free tier they can test first, then upgrade when they trust it

**What causes hesitation:**
- "I'll just set up the free version and see if I get alerts" (defers upgrade)
- "My current plugin is working fine, I don't need to switch"
- "This feels complicated — maybe I should just hire someone"
- Price sensitivity, especially for bloggers

**Purchase drivers for each persona:**
- Solo blogger: Emotional reassurance. "You'll finally understand what the alerts mean."
- WooCommerce owner: ROI framing. "One prevented hack saves 10x the annual price."
- Freelancer: Efficiency. "Manage all your clients' security from one dashboard."

---

## Stage 5: Post-Purchase

**Immediate success signals (first 7 days):**
- The dashboard shows real detections — proves it's working
- First queue item appears and they understand what it's asking
- They approve or dismiss an action and feel confident in the decision
- No site breakage

**30-day retention signals:**
- Weekly vulnerability scan runs, shows results
- At least one fix has been approved and executed successfully
- They've told someone about it ("I found this plugin that explains security in plain English")

**Renewal triggers (annual):**
- Active threats detected in the past year — plugin proved its value
- Site has been safe — peace of mind has value
- They remember a previous hack or incident — ShieldAI is insurance

**Churn triggers:**
- Never saw any detections — "must not need it"
- Too many false positives — approval fatigue
- Price increase without visible value improvement
- Switched to a managed WordPress host that includes security

## Channel Strategy Recommendations

**Priority 1 — SEO and Content**
Target "wordfence vs sucuri vs shieldai" comparison queries and "how to fix [specific vulnerability]" queries. Each agent output maps to a content piece.

**Priority 2 — WordPress.org Plugin Directory**
The free tier listed on WordPress.org is the primary organic acquisition channel for this market. 5M Wordfence downloads proves the channel works. Every plugin description page is a conversion opportunity.

**Priority 3 — YouTube Tutorials**
A video showing the approval queue UI — a real security fix being proposed, reviewed, and approved — will convert better than any written content. The product sells itself visually.

**Priority 4 — Community Seeding**
Answer security questions on Reddit and in Facebook groups. Not sales pitches — genuine help that positions ShieldAI as the thoughtful choice. The founder being visible in these communities builds trust.

**Priority 5 — Partner Reviews**
A single positive review article on WPBeginner can drive thousands of installs. Outreach to major WordPress review sites for hands-on testing is a high-leverage investment.

**Priority 6 — Email Nurture (Free Users)**
The free tier email list is the most valuable owned asset. Free users who see a detection they can't act on (because it requires Shield plan) are warm upgrade candidates. Automated sequences showing what the queue looks like convert well.
