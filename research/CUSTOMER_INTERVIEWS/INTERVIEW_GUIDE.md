# ShieldAI Customer Discovery Interview Guide
**Phase 3a — Behavioral Research | Customer Interviews**
**Version:** 1.0 | **Created:** 2026-04-09

---

## 1. Interview Overview

### Purpose
Understand how WordPress site owners currently think about, experience, and respond to security threats — before presenting any product concept. Surface real behaviors, workarounds, fears, and decision criteria that will validate or challenge ShieldAI's core assumptions.

### Research Goals
- Map the real security workflow of each participant segment (bloggers, WooCommerce owners, freelancers/agencies)
- Identify where trust breaks down with existing security tools
- Understand the emotional stakes attached to a compromised site
- Determine what "good enough" looks like to each segment — and where "good enough" still fails them
- Gauge appetite for AI-generated, owner-approved automated remediation
- Gather willingness-to-pay signals with enough context to be credible

### What This Is NOT
- A demo or pitch session. Do not sell.
- A survey. Follow threads. Let the participant lead.
- An opportunity to validate your assumptions by asking leading questions.

### Duration
45–60 minutes

### Format
- Remote video call (camera on preferred) or in-person
- One interviewer, one note-taker (where possible)
- Record audio/video with explicit consent

### Recording & Consent
Read the following aloud before beginning:

> "Before we start, I want to confirm a couple of things. First, everything you share today is for internal research only — your name and site will not be published or attributed anywhere. Second, I'd like to record this session so I can review it later rather than trying to write everything down while we talk. Is that okay with you? You can ask me to pause or stop the recording at any time."

Confirm verbal consent is captured on the recording. If the participant declines, proceed without recording and rely on notes.

## 2. Participant Screening Criteria

Recruit participants who meet ALL criteria in their respective tier. Target 5–8 interviews per tier, minimum 3 per tier before drawing segment-level conclusions.

### Tier A — Independent Bloggers / Content Creators
- Personally manage at least one self-hosted WordPress site (not WordPress.com)
- Site has been live for 12+ months
- Not employed as a developer or IT professional
- Updates plugins/themes at least occasionally (not fully delegated)
- Site receives at least 500 monthly visitors (ensures real stakes)

### Tier B — WooCommerce Store Owners
- Run an active WooCommerce store with at least 10 transactions in the past 90 days
- Personally manage or closely oversee the WordPress installation
- Not a developer by trade (may use a developer occasionally)
- Store processes real payments (not a test/demo store)
- Familiar with the concept of PCI compliance or customer data responsibility, even loosely

### Tier C — Freelancers / Agency Owners Managing Client Sites
- Actively manage WordPress sites for 3+ clients currently
- Responsible for security posture on those sites (not just design/content)
- Experienced at least one client site incident or security scare
- Regularly installs, evaluates, or recommends security plugins
- Comfortable discussing client relationships and how security gets communicated

### Disqualify If
- Currently employed at a WordPress security company or plugin vendor
- Works exclusively with managed WordPress hosts that handle all security (e.g., WP Engine fully managed plans where they do nothing)
- Has fewer than 6 months of WordPress site management experience
- Agreed to participate but hasn't actually managed a security incident, update cycle, or plugin evaluation in the past year

## 3. Warm-Up Questions

_Goal: Build rapport, establish context for later questions, get the participant talking freely. Do not rush this section — 8–10 minutes is appropriate._

**3.1** Tell me a little about yourself and how you got started with WordPress.

**3.2** Walk me through the sites you manage right now. How many, roughly what kind, and how long have they been running?

**3.3** Who else, if anyone, is involved in keeping those sites running? Do you have a developer you call, a hosting support team, anyone like that?

**3.4** On a typical week, how much time do you spend on the technical upkeep of your site — things like updates, backups, checking on things?

**3.5** _(For Tier C only)_ When a client asks you about security, how do you usually handle that conversation?

## 4. SPIN-Based Core Questions

_Work through these sections in order. Each section transitions naturally into the next. The goal is to surface the problem before exploring implications — never jump ahead._

---

### 4S — Situation

_Understand the current environment. Keep it factual and grounded. Do not probe for pain yet._

**4S.1** What hosting provider are you on, and do you know roughly what security features they provide at the infrastructure level?

**4S.2** Walk me through the security plugins or tools you have installed right now. How did you end up with that particular setup?

**4S.3** How do you currently handle WordPress core, plugin, and theme updates? Do you have a routine, or does it happen when you notice something?

**4S.4** Do you run any kind of regular backup? If so, where do backups go and how often?

**4S.5** Have you ever looked at the logs or activity reports from your security plugin? What do you usually see there?

**4S.6** _(If using a managed service or security plugin)_ When was the last time you actually logged into that security tool and checked something? What were you looking at?

---

### 4P — Problem

_Surface frustrations, anxieties, and gaps. Let silence work. Do not offer solutions._

**4P.1** What's the most frustrating part of managing security on your WordPress site?

**4P.2** When your security plugin sends you an alert or notification, what do you usually do with it?

**4P.3** Have there been times you got a warning or notification and had no idea what to do next? What happened?

**4P.4** How confident are you that your site is actually secure right now — on a scale of 1 to 10? What's the gap between where you are and a 10?

**4P.5** Is there anything about WordPress security that you feel like you should be doing but aren't? Why aren't you doing it?

**4P.6** What's the thing that scares you most when it comes to your site being attacked or compromised?

**4P.7** _(Probe deeper if they mention a past incident)_ Tell me more about that. What exactly happened, and what was your first reaction?

---

### 4I — Implication

_Explore the downstream consequences of those problems. Make the cost of inaction visible without being manipulative — just ask what actually happens._

**4I.1** If your site went down or got hacked tomorrow, what would the immediate impact be on your business or income?

**4I.2** Has a security issue — a hack, a malware flag, a blacklisting — ever actually happened to you or to a site you manage? What was the fallout?

**4I.3** What would it cost you, in time or money, to recover from a serious compromise? Have you ever had to estimate that?

**4I.4** _(For WooCommerce owners)_ If your store was compromised and customer payment data was involved, what would that mean for you?

**4I.5** _(For freelancers)_ If a client site you managed got hacked, how would that affect your relationship with that client?

**4I.6** How much time in the past month have you spent dealing with security-related tasks — alerts, updates, worrying, researching a problem? What could you have done with that time instead?

**4I.7** Have security concerns ever caused you to delay launching something, avoid installing a plugin you wanted, or hold back on a feature? Tell me about that.

---

### 4N — Need-Payoff

_Shift toward what the participant values. Let them articulate the solution — do not describe your product yet._

**4N.1** If you could wave a magic wand and change one thing about how WordPress security works, what would it be?

**4N.2** What would "security sorted" look like for you — what would your day-to-day be like if you never had to worry about this again?

**4N.3** When you think about a security tool that actually works well, what does it do that existing tools don't?

**4N.4** How important is it to you that you understand what a security fix is doing before it runs on your site? Or would you rather it just handle things automatically?

**4N.5** If a tool could detect a threat and present you with a plain-English explanation of what happened and exactly what it would do to fix it — before taking any action — would that change how much you trust it? Why or why not?

**4N.6** What would make you actually open and read a security alert, rather than ignore it or dismiss it?

## 5. Behavioral Research Questions

_This section digs into actual past behavior, not hypothetical preferences. Behavior is more reliable than stated preference. Probe for specifics — dates, tools used, what they actually clicked, how they felt._

---

### 5A — Past Security Incidents

**5A.1** Tell me about the last time something went wrong on one of your sites — even something minor, like a suspicious login or a plugin flagging something. Walk me through exactly what happened.

**5A.2** How did you find out there was a problem? What was the first sign?

**5A.3** What did you do first? Who did you contact, what did you Google, what tools did you use?

**5A.4** How long did it take to resolve? What was the hardest part of resolving it?

**5A.5** After it was over, did you change anything about how you manage your site? What specifically?

**5A.6** Has there ever been a moment where you thought your site was compromised but turned out it wasn't? What caused the false alarm, and how did you react?

---

### 5B — Workarounds They Use Today

**5B.1** Are there things you do manually to compensate for gaps in your security tools — things you do out of habit because the tools don't cover it?

**5B.2** Do you have any informal checks you run — like Googling your site for malware flags, or checking Google Search Console for manual actions?

**5B.3** Have you ever delayed a plugin update because you were worried it would break something? How do you decide when it's safe to update?

**5B.4** If you're not sure whether a plugin is safe or trustworthy, what do you do to check?

**5B.5** Do you keep notes, a checklist, or any kind of document tracking what security steps you've taken? If not, how do you remember what you've done?

---

### 5C — How They Decide to Buy a Plugin

**5C.1** Think about the last security plugin or tool you purchased or installed. How did you find it?

**5C.2** What made you trust it enough to install it on your site? What would have made you not trust it?

**5C.3** Did you try a free version first, or did you go straight to paid? What tipped you toward paying?

**5C.4** What made you skeptical? Was there anything about the plugin that gave you pause before installing?

**5C.5** Have you ever uninstalled a security plugin? What caused you to leave?

**5C.6** When you're comparing two plugins that seem similar, what factors do you actually weigh?

**5C.7** How much do reviews, star ratings, and active install counts matter to your decision? Do you read the negative reviews?

---

### 5D — What They Wish Security Tools Did Differently

**5D.1** What do current security plugins do that you find genuinely useful? Be specific.

**5D.2** What do they do that wastes your time or feels pointless?

**5D.3** Do you feel like security plugins talk to you in a language you understand, or do they assume technical knowledge you don't have?

**5D.4** If you could redesign the way a security plugin communicates with you — alerts, reports, recommendations — what would change?

**5D.5** Have you ever wished a security tool would just handle something without requiring you to figure out what to do? What was the situation?

**5D.6** On the flip side, have you ever been uncomfortable with a plugin taking action on your site that you didn't explicitly approve? What happened?

---

## 6. Concept Validation

_Present the ShieldAI concept only after the previous sections are complete. The participant's own words from the interview should already be ringing in their ears when they hear this. Present neutrally — do not oversell._

### Concept Brief (Read or Show)

> "I want to describe a WordPress security plugin concept and get your honest reaction — including anything that sounds off or hard to believe. Here's how it works:
>
> The plugin monitors your site continuously for threats — malware, suspicious logins, vulnerable plugins, configuration problems. When it detects something, it doesn't just send you an alert. Instead, an AI analyzes the specific threat and generates a targeted fix for your site. It then presents that fix to you in plain English: here's what's wrong, here's exactly what we'd do to fix it, here's what could go wrong. You review it, you approve it, and then it executes. Nothing happens on your site without your go-ahead.
>
> That's the core idea: AI does the detection and diagnosis, you make the call, the plugin does the work."

---

**6.1** What's your first reaction to that?

**6.2** What part of that resonates most with problems you've described having?

**6.3** What part of that sounds too good to be true, or makes you skeptical?

**6.4** When I said "an AI generates a specific fix for your site" — what went through your mind? What concerns does that raise?

**6.5** The model here is that nothing runs on your site without your explicit approval. Is that reassuring, or does it feel like it puts too much responsibility back on you?

**6.6** Who would you trust to build and maintain something like this? What would they need to demonstrate to earn that trust?

**6.7** Can you imagine a scenario where you'd use this and something would still go wrong? What would that look like?

**6.8** How does this compare to what you're using today? What would you have to give up, and what would you gain?

**6.9** Is there anyone you'd want to share something like this with — a client, a colleague, a community?

---

## 7. Pricing Willingness

_Keep this conversational, not transactional. Anchor to value they've already described, not features. The goal is a range and the logic behind it, not a hard number._

**7.1** You mentioned that [reference specific cost or time loss they described earlier]. Given that context, what would it be worth to you to have this problem solved reliably?

**7.2** If this plugin cost you $X per month [insert a number slightly above your target price], what would your reaction be? Would you need to think about it, or is that an easy yes or no?

**7.3** What pricing model makes sense to you for a tool like this — monthly subscription, annual plan, per site, per number of sites? Why?

**7.4** _(For freelancers)_ Would you pay for this yourself and roll it into client costs, or would you expect clients to pay for it directly?

**7.5** Is there a price at which this becomes a no-brainer, and a price at which you'd say "that's too much for what it is"? What are those numbers for you?

**7.6** Have you ever paid for a WordPress security plugin and felt like you got your money's worth? What made it worth it?

---

## 8. Wrap-Up

_Reserve 5 minutes. Close with genuine curiosity, not a sales close._

**8.1** Is there anything about your experience with WordPress security that I didn't ask about that feels important?

**8.2** If you were advising the team building this kind of plugin, what's the one thing you'd tell them not to get wrong?

**8.3** Do you know other WordPress site owners — bloggers, store owners, freelancers — who might be worth talking to? Could we reach out to them?

**8.4** Would you be open to a follow-up conversation once we have something more concrete to show you?

---

_Thank the participant. Confirm next steps if applicable. Stop the recording._
